## What Counts as a Pre-Existing Condition?

A **pre-existing condition** is any illness, injury, or medical condition that existed before your health insurance policy's effective date. This includes conditions you're currently treating, conditions you've had in the past, and even conditions you may not know about.

### Common Examples of Pre-Existing Conditions

Insurance providers typically look for these conditions during **medical underwriting**:

- **Chronic diseases:** Diabetes (Type 1 and Type 2), hypertension, asthma, thyroid disorders
- **Heart conditions:** Arrhythmia, previous heart attack, heart disease
- **Cancer:** Current diagnosis or history of cancer (remission status matters)
- **Mental health:** Depression, anxiety disorders, bipolar disorder
- **Kidney conditions:** Chronic kidney disease, kidney stones history
- **Autoimmune diseases:** Lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis
- **Previous surgeries:** Organ transplants, major surgeries with lasting effects

### How Insurers Identify Pre-Existing Conditions

Insurance companies use several methods to identify pre-existing conditions:

1. **Medical Questionnaire:** Every application includes questions about your health history. You'll list past diagnoses, treatments, hospitalizations, and current medications.

2. **Medical Examination:** Some plans require a physical exam, blood tests, or imaging. Results can reveal conditions you haven't disclosed or weren't aware of.

3. **Medical Records Request:** For certain applications, insurers may request records from your doctors or hospitals.

4. **Claims History:** If you're transferring from another plan, your claims history may be reviewed.

💡 **Tagalog Tip:** *Kung may tinatago kang sakit o nakalimutan mong condition, mas mabuti pang aminin mo agad. Ang mga insurance companies ay may access sa medical records at kung ma-discover nila ang hindi mo sinabi, pwedeng ma-deny ang claim mo kahit hindi related ang condition.*

## How Health Insurance Handles Pre-Existing Conditions

Understanding how insurance providers handle pre-existing conditions helps you set realistic expectations. Here are the three main approaches:

### 1. Waiting Periods

The most common approach is a **waiting period** — a specific timeframe during which your pre-existing condition isn't covered. Once the waiting period ends, the condition becomes part of your regular coverage.

**Standard waiting periods in the Philippines:**
- **6 months** for minor conditions
- **12 months** for most chronic conditions
- **24 months** for more serious conditions (varies by provider)

During the waiting period, you're still covered for *other* illnesses and accidents — just not your pre-existing condition. This is why it's crucial to apply early, before any condition worsens.

### 2. Permanent Exclusions

Some conditions may be permanently excluded from coverage. This means the insurer will never pay for treatment related to that specific condition.

**Conditions often permanently excluded:**
- Certain cancers with high recurrence risk
- Advanced chronic kidney disease
- Terminal illnesses
- Pre-existing conditions requiring ongoing expensive treatment

Even with exclusions, you still get coverage for everything else — accidents, new illnesses, and unrelated medical needs.

### 3. Premium Loading

For certain conditions, insurers may offer coverage but at a higher premium. This is called **premium loading** — essentially, you pay more because you're higher risk.

Premium loading varies by provider:
- Some charge 20–50% extra for controlled diabetes
- Others may charge 100%+ for more serious conditions
- A few providers don't offer loading at all — they either accept or exclude

The key is understanding that premium loading isn't punishment; it's actuarial reality. The insurer is taking on known risk, and pricing accordingly.

## Strategies for Getting Coverage with Pre-Existing Conditions

Getting approved with a pre-existing condition requires strategy. Here's what works:

### 1. Full Disclosure — Always

Never hide a condition. Non-disclosure is the #1 reason claims get denied. Even if your condition seems minor or "under control," disclose it.

Insurance companies investigate claims thoroughly. If you file a claim and they discover you had a condition you didn't report, they can deny the claim and cancel your policy — even if the claim is unrelated.

### 2. Apply While Young and Conditions Are Controlled

The best time to get insurance is when you're relatively healthy. If you have a chronic condition, apply when it's well-controlled:

- **Diabetes:** Good HbA1c levels show management
- **Hypertension:** Consistent normal blood pressure readings help
- **Asthma:** No recent attacks or hospitalizations demonstrates stability

Your medical records during the application period matter. A doctor's note confirming good management can strengthen your application.

### 3. Prepare Your Medical Records

Gather relevant medical documents before applying:
- Recent lab results
- Doctor's certification of condition management
- Hospital discharge summaries (if applicable)
- List of current medications

Complete documentation speeds up **medical underwriting** and shows you're organized and transparent.

Independent agents (like me) work with multiple providers and know which ones are more favorable for specific conditions. We can:

- Pre-screen your application with underwriters (informally)
- Recommend providers based on your specific condition
- Help present your medical history in the most favorable light
- Advocate for you during the underwriting process

💡 **Tagalog Tip:** *Huwag mong hintayin na lumala ang condition mo bago ka mag-apply. Kapag stable at well-controlled ang sakit mo, mas malaki ang chance na ma-approve ka. ### 5. Consider a Waiting Period Waiver

Some group plans (employer-sponsored) offer **waiting period waiver** for pre-existing conditions. If you're employed and your company offers health insurance, this could be an option.

## What Happens If You Don't Disclose

Non-disclosure has serious consequences. Here's what can happen:

### 1. Claim Denial

If you file a claim and the insurer discovers an undisclosed condition, they will deny the claim — even if the claim is unrelated to your pre-existing condition.

**Real example:** A client (let's call her Maria) didn't disclose she had thyroid surgery 5 years ago. When she filed a claim for dengue hospitalization, the insurer discovered the surgery during their investigation. Her claim was denied, and her policy was cancelled for non-disclosure. She lost coverage for a condition completely unrelated to her thyroid.

### 2. Policy Cancellation

Insurance contracts have a "utmost good faith" principle. By not disclosing, you violate this principle, giving insurers grounds to cancel your policy entirely.

### 3. Financial Loss

You lose all premiums paid, plus you're now without coverage — potentially when you need it most.

### 4. Difficulty Getting Future Coverage

A policy cancellation for non-disclosure goes on record. Future insurers will see this, making it harder (and more expensive) to get coverage elsewhere.

## FAQ

### Can I get health insurance with pre-existing conditions in the Philippines?

Yes, you can get health insurance with pre-existing conditions. Most Philippine providers offer coverage with waiting periods (typically 6–12 months) before your pre-existing condition becomes covered. Some conditions may have permanent exclusions or premium loading, but having a condition doesn't make you uninsurable.

### What is the standard waiting period for pre-existing conditions?

The standard waiting period is 6–12 months in the Philippines. Minor conditions often have 6-month waiting periods, while chronic conditions like diabetes or hypertension typically have 12-month waiting periods. More serious conditions may require 24 months or result in permanent exclusion.

### Will my premium be higher if I have a pre-existing condition?

Possibly. Some providers apply premium loading (higher rates) for certain conditions. This varies by provider — some charge 20–50% extra for controlled diabetes, while others don't offer loading at all. Always ask about premium impact before finalizing your application.

### What happens if I don't disclose my medical history?

Non-disclosure can result in claim denial, policy cancellation, and difficulty getting future coverage. Insurers investigate claims thoroughly, and discovering an undisclosed condition — even one unrelated to your claim — gives them grounds to deny coverage and cancel your policy.

### Can I appeal a pre-existing condition exclusion?

Yes, you can request reconsideration, especially if your condition improves or you have new medical evidence. Some providers allow appeals after a certain period (often 1–2 years) of demonstrated good health management. Success varies by provider and condition.

### Which Philippine health insurance is best for pre-existing conditions?

Pacific Cross is often considered most flexible for pre-existing conditions, with case-by-case underwriting that considers overall health management. Etiqa offers clear, predictable policies. The "best" provider depends on your specific condition, budget, and coverage needs — working with a licensed agent can help identify your best option.
